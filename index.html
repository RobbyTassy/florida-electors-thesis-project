Timeline
  • Early November

    Registered voters cast their votes for President and Vice President. By doing so, they also help choose the electors who will represent their state in the Electoral College.

  • Mid-November

    The United States Constitution and Federal law do not prescribe the method of appointment other than requiring that electors must be appointed on the Tuesday after the first Monday in After the presidential election, the governor of your state prepares seven Certificates of Ascertainment. “As soon as practicable,” after the election results in your state are certified, the governor sends one of the Certificates of Ascertainment to the Archivist.

  • Early December

    States must make final decisions in any controversies over the appointment of their electors at least six days before the meeting of the Electors on December 17, 2012. This is so their electoral votes will be presumed valid when presented to Congress. The deadline for resolving any controversies is December 11, 2012.

  • 09 May 2013

    The Electors meet in their state and vote for President and Vice President on separate ballots. The electors record their votes on six “Certificates of Vote,” which are paired with the six remaining Certificates of Ascertainment.

  • 08 May 2013

    The deadline for receipt of the electoral votes by the President of the Senate and the Archivist is December 26, 2012. States face no legal penalty for failure to comply. The Archivist and/or representatives from the Office of the Federal Register meet with the Secretary of the Senate and the Clerk of the House in late December or early January. This is, in part, a ceremonial occasion. Informal meetings may take place earlier.

  • 08 May 2013

    The Congress meets in joint session to count the electoral votes. The Vice President, as President of the Senate, presides over the count and announces the results of the Electoral College vote. The President of the Senate then declares which persons, if any, have been elected President and Vice President of the United States.

  • 13 May 2013

    The President-elect takes the Oath of Office and becomes the President of the United States.

Electoral College votes every presidential election can be described as Florida’s 29 swing votes, California’s 55 blue votes, and Texas' 38 red votes on prime time news shows, but perhaps, to some surprise, these votes also have a human face.

Listed as Florida’s 25th elector is Bob Troy, retired architect and political activist. On election day in November, Troy was out in his neighborhood knocking on doors and making phone calls getting as many registered Democrats to show up to the polls as possible.

"Florida has more registered Democrats than any other state," said Sherrye, Bob's wife. "But they don't show up to the polls."

Bob, with the company of his wife, and along with 28 others served as electors for Florida in the 2012 presidential election.

Electors like Bob Troy and Jean Monestime from Florida are chosen to recognize their service and dedication to their political party. They may be State-elected officials, party leaders, or persons who have a personal or political affiliation with the Presidential candidate.

“It was a high-strung event,” Bob recalls when describing his ballot casting in the shiny halls of the Florida Senate chambers. “I was nervous up until actually casting the ballot.”

His vote along with co-elector Chairman Jean Monestime's proved to both to be a worthwhile experience in a race where re-election for the incumbent was not seemingly promising.

“Republicans said they were going to make him a one-term president," Sherrye said. "So it was exciting to experience him getting re-elected."

Florida, in particular, voted in favor of the democratic candidate for the second presidential election year in a row and for the first time since 1948. Based on the winner-take-all system, this allowed all 29 of Florida's electoral votes to “unofficially” go to democratic candidate and incumbent Barack Obama.

Celebrating his candidate’s recent victory, Troy received a phone call at home asking if he would serve as an elector for Florida; his efforts to register voters in his neighborhood, push them out to the polls, and build a grassroots volunteer force in his community had not gone unnoticed by the Obama campaign. Troy and 28 others would accept this token of party appreciation.

"We quickly established a rapport with the state party," said Bob in regards to how he was chosen to be an elector. "A rapport that we were people you could depend on."

Dina Heffernan, a sales publishing professional from Boca Raton, also served as a neighborhood volunteer and team leader with the Obama campaign to begin a grassroots efforts in her community.

"We hosted many house parties," said Heffernan. "Where we met to discuss strategy and what needed to get done."

Similar to Bob, Heffernan received a call from the Obama Campaign and Florida Democratic Party leaders letting her known she had also been chosen to be an elector representing Florida.

"My first response was 'what's an elector?'" recalled Heffernan.

She would not only learn what an elector is, but she would experience it firsthand when she and Troy gather in Tallahassee in December to formally cast their ballot for president; the only ballots that constitutionally elect the President of the United States.

Florida’s 29 electoral votes taken from congressional numbers and apportioned every 10 years, has attained four additional seats since the previous census. Meanwhile states like New York have lost two house seats, and in effect, lost two electoral college votes despite its increase in the state's population.

This representation gap has long been attributed to the 435 limit in house seats that, since 1940, has remained fixed in American government despite 60 percent increase in population. The stick figures in the graphic above represent each elector that would cast their vote for president. In the case of Florida, 29 electors represent a constituent base of over 18 million. Nonetheless, state electors chosen every fourth year are chosen across the nation whether it's just three electors from Vermont or as many as 29 electors from New York.

In Bob and Sherrye's case, they were chosen for their unique contribution of being the original boots on the ground. Their success as volunteers and team leaders made it possible for other grassroot efforts to spread throughout Miami-Dade county.

“We were the initial team that other teams broke off of in the whole county,” Sherrye recalls. “The first team in all Miami-Dade county.”

The Troys led and trained dozens of other volunteer groups to build their own centralized effort back in their respective local neighborhoods to register voters, call registered Democrats, and knock on doors. Many doors.

It was a chilly day in Florida weather when Bob and Sherrye found themselves heading to a different kind of poll to vote for president. This 'poll' was was in Tallahassee in the grand Florida Senate chambers.

Although Bob was formally selected as the elector, he could not have done it all himself his without the support of his wife of 54 years.

“You could only have one person with you when you cast the ballot,” Bob explained. “I’m taking my co-team leader and my wife, Sherrye.”

Bob Troy was among 29 electors who cast their ballot for president on December 17, 2012. Although Bob was the selected elector, his hard work during the campaign to get President Barack Obama re-elected landed him an opportunity to cast his ballot for the candidate a second time -- this time, an electoral ballot.

Bob was the designated driver for their trip to Tallahassee.

They headed first to the state party celebration luncheon the day of casting their ballots in the senate chambers. The lunch was paid for by the Florida Democratic Party, they were greeted by fellow electors and ranking members of the party, among them Rod Smith, former chairman of the party, who was also chosen as one of the 29 electors.

Bob had a burger that day. But it could not taste like regular burger for reasons no one else could understand but him: he had a feeling of discomfort and anxiety, a 'what-if' feeling because immediately after the luncheon electors were to assemble at the state capitol to vote.

“If you’re anything like me, you’re thinking: what if the car breaks down, what if you get lost.” Troy said. “It’s not like you’re a veteran when you do this.”

After mild interaction with others and forcefully swallowing his meal for the day, Bob and Sherrye were back in their car to drive to the state capitol.

Bob was just under the speed limit driving his car to the capitol even though he was well on schedule. He parked before entering through the golden circulating archways of the Florida Senate chambers into the reception area where he was greeted with a smile by Ken Detzner.

Bob and Sherrye were escorted to the shiny, wooden tiled floors of the Florida Senate room where only one immediate guest was permitted on the floor, additional guests were allowed in balcony overhang above.

After people gathered on the floor and pictures were taken, the meeting was called to order by State Secretary Ken Detzner with welcoming remarks and a roll of each elector. The meeting was swift. Electors were called one by one to walk up to the voting table and check off a candidate's name on a paper ballot in what took almost an hour; this time the only candidate slate on the ballot was: 'Barack Obama' and 'Joe Biden'.

After moving along the table signing multiple copies of identical presidential ballots, electors ended the routine by voting orally and stating the name of the candidate for which they voted:

"Barack Obama," exclaimed Troy with a subtle level of pride and joy.

Simultaneously across the country, other state electors also cast their electoral votes for the candidate who won their state's popular vote. The votes are collected by the state secretary, sent to the vice president of the United States - who also serves as the president of the Senate - by the deadline for formal counting of the electoral votes and announcement of winning candidate. The winning candidate is sworn into office in late January.

Bob and Sherrye returned to enjoy the warmth of their home in Kendall after their long presidential journey working hours to re-elect the president both as campaign volunteers and electors. As another token of appreciation, they were also invited to attend the inauguration. They declined due to cold weather.

Instead they attended a local watch party luncheon with their South Dade Democrats club members and shared exciting stories of their journey with friends.

Bob and Sherrye plan to still remain politically active.

“But we won’t be back as team leaders,” They said looking ahead at 2016. “It’s time to pass the baton to some younger people.”
